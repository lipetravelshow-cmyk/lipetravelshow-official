Travel insurance: how to choose well in 2026

Travel insurance is not a bureaucratic add-on. It is part of real trip planning — especially for Europe, the U.S., frequent travel, family trips, expensive baggage, pregnancy, pre-existing conditions, or anyone who wants to reduce the impact of medical issues, delays and lost luggage.

What I check before clicking “buy”

  • Whether the medical coverage makes sense for the destination and local healthcare costs.
  • Whether the plan matches Schengen requirements when Europe is part of the trip.
  • Whether my profile calls for single-trip, annual multi-trip or On Trip coverage.
  • Whether pregnancy, pre-existing conditions, recreational sports and baggage are addressed.
  • Whether limits and exclusions are clearly stated in the voucher and terms.

Europe

If you are entering the Schengen area, checking the €30,000 minimum medical coverage is a basic step.

U.S. and high-cost destinations

The higher the medical costs at destination, the less sense it makes to underinsure your trip.

Frequent traveller

If you take two or more trips a year, compare single-trip pricing with an annual multi-trip plan.

Already travelling

There is a dedicated option for travellers who forgot insurance or extended their trip.

How to choose

The right logic is not “the cheapest.” It is the right plan for your trip profile.

In practice, I would break the decision into four questions: where you are going, how often you travel each year, whether you have already departed, and whether there is any special condition in the trip that deserves extra attention.

1

Destination

Europe, the U.S., South America, Asia or multiple destinations change the coverage benchmark.

2

Duration

A one-off vacation is one logic. Year-round travel may justify an annual plan.

3

Timing of purchase

Buying before departure is one scenario. Buying after you are already abroad is another.

4

Traveller profile

Pregnancy, age, pre-existing conditions, sports, baggage and electronics can all matter.

Coverage profiles

Which type of travel insurance fits each trip best

Single-trip plan

Best for one specific trip with fixed dates. This is the natural choice for vacations, honeymoons, family trips, eurotrips or a single business trip.

  • Ideal for a clearly scheduled departure.
  • Lets you align coverage with destination.
  • Worth comparing price against actual trip needs.

Annual multi-trip

Best for people who travel often for leisure or business. Assist Card officially presents annual coverage with multiple departures, subject to a maximum of 30 or 60 days per individual trip depending on the plan selected.

  • A strong fit for people taking 2+ trips per year.
  • More practical than buying insurance over and over.
  • Requires attention to the trip-length limit.

On Trip

This is the option designed for travellers who are already abroad or who extended their stay. Assist Card states a 72-hour waiting period for claims related to illnesses; accidents are treated differently.

  • Useful if you forgot to buy before departure.
  • Useful if your trip became longer than planned.
  • Assist Card currently presents On Trip 50 and 100 options.

Europe, Schengen and 2026

For Europe, treat travel insurance as part of the trip, not paperwork

The European documentation reviewed for this page still indicates a minimum of €30,000 in travel medical insurance coverage valid throughout the Schengen area and for the full duration of stay. For Europe-bound trips, this is one of the first checks I would make.

Quick Schengen checklist

  • Coverage that meets the minimum threshold.
  • Validity for the entire travel period.
  • Coverage valid throughout the Schengen area.
  • Voucher and policy details accessible on your phone.

My practical take

Even when insurance looks like a formal requirement, it is also operational protection. A simple medical event abroad, a major delay or a luggage issue can easily cost more than the savings from buying the wrong plan.

FAQ

Common questions before you buy

Is travel insurance the same as health insurance?

No. Assist Card itself warns that travel insurance is not health insurance. What matters is the exact set of coverages, limits, exclusions and conditions in the selected plan.

Can I buy it after I have already departed?

For standard international travel, purchase should happen before the trip begins. If you are already travelling, Assist Card directs travellers to the On Trip option.

Should frequent travellers look at annual plans?

Yes. For people who travel repeatedly, annual multi-trip coverage is often a much more logical product than buying a separate voucher every single time.

Can I rely only on my credit card insurance?

You can consider it, but compare carefully: medical limits, baggage, cancellation, duration, eligibility, ticket-payment rules and actual service access. Card coverage is not always enough for your real trip profile.
